Alan Turing (1912 - 1954)

1912

Alan Turing is born in Maida Vale, London.

When Alan was born, his father, Julius Mathison Turing was on leave from his position with the Indian Civil Service at Chhatrapur, Bihar and Orissa Province (British India).

1918

At the age of six, his parents enrolled him in St Michael's, a day school at 20 Charles Road, St Leonards-on-Sea.

The headmistress of the school, and many others, recognized his talent for science and mathematics.

1922

Alan continues his education at Hazelhurst Preparatory School. an independent school in the village of Frant in Sussex (now East Sussex).

An independent school in the village of Frant in Sussex (now East Sussex).

1926

At age 13, Alan moves onto Sherborne School, an independent school in the market town of Sherborne in Dorset.

Alan Turing was so determined to attend his first day at his new school, despite the the 1926 General Strike, that he rode his bicycle unaccompanied for 60 miles, whilst only stopping at an overnight inn.

1928

Turing encounters Albert Einstein's work.

It's be deduced that not only did Alan Turing grasp Albert Einsteins work at the age of 16, but it was possible that he manage to surmise Einstein's questioning of Newton's laws of motion.

1930

Loses his friend Christopher Morcom to complications with bovine tuberculosis.

This event caused Turing to become ridden with grief in which he coped by working harder on topics related to science and mathematics; both of which he shared with his friend.

1931

Alan Turing begin his university years by becoming an undergraduate at King's College, Cambridge to study Mathematics.

He is able to gain first-class honors in mathematics.

1935

At age 22, he is elected a fellow of king's based off his dissertation proving the central limit theorem.

Unknown to the committee, the theorem had already been proven by Jarl Waldemar Lindeberg

1936

Publishes a paper "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to Entscheidungsproblem" and begins to spend more time studying under Church at Princeton University.

This paper reformulates Kurt Godel's 1931 results on the limits of proof and computation and thus replaces Godel's universal arithmetic-based formulas with hypothetical devices known as Turing machines. In addition, he studied cryptology and built 3/4 of an electro-mechanical binary multiplier.

1938

Obtains his PhD from Princeton.

John Von Neumann wants to hire Alan Turing as his postdoctoral assistant but was declined.

1939

Alan reports to Bletchley Park, the wartime station of the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS), specifying the bombe.

The bombe was the first of five major cryptanalytical advances that Turing made during World War II.

1942

Alan and his colleagues manage to break the German Naval Enigma System.

This breakthrough helps the allies tremendously in the Battle of the Atlantic.

1943

Is asked to work as a top-level intelligence link with the USA.

He agrees and visits to share information on cryptology.

1945

The war ends.

Alan recieves the Order of the British Empire award (OBE) for his wartime services.

1946

Alan joins the National Physical Laboratory, in Teddington.

Alan publishes a paper with the first detailed design of a stored-program computer.

1950

Alan publishes'Computing Machinery and Intelligence'

From this, he develops the Turing Test, which is an attempt to define a standard for a machine to be called intelligent.

1951

Alan is elected Fellow of the Royal Society.

He also went on BBC radio and gave a talk on Artifical Intelligence.

1952

Arrested for gross indecency.

He losses his security clearance and is offerred chemical treatment in place of prision time. This hormone treatment has a very negative effect on his body.

1954

Alan is found dead in his home in Wilmslow Cheshire.

Autopsy specifies his dead was due to poisoning by cyanide. A half eaten apple laced with the same poison is deemed the delievery weapon.
